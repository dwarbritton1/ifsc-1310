Dragon/Segway Project Revision and Reflection

For iteration one of the Dragon/Segway project, I sought the feedback of a programmer friend, as well as a graphic design friend. They both indicated that the dark grey in the background was "kinda depressing", which is a critique that I agree with. At first, I had sought to give the page a sort of technological sheen and plan to revise the color scheme slightly. They also indicated that the buttons were kind of small and that I should perhaps shorten "Segway Centaur" to "Centaur", as it's redundant.

As far as things that I would personally do differently, I'm definitely on the same page as my friends. I'm going to attempt to go for a more sleek design, with a slightly less downer color palette. Additionally, regarding the full process, I would structure the content to have more of an emphasis on graphics and images. This would help aid in potentially selling the product. I've attempted to find more high resolution photos of the Centaur concept, but it seems to have become vaporware.

Thoughts on Progressive Enhancement

Oddly enough, I feel as if I've been unconciously following this philosophy the whole semester. I have a tendency to approach projects from the bottom up. First step is to define what the actual content is. For example, when parsing the Segway html prompt for logical places to add tags, it was quite a nit-picky endeavor for me, especially with regards to the features/concept/design/environment sections.

One segment of the article that really stood out to me was the Peanut M&Ms analogy. Content is the protein, presentation is the chocolate sweetness that draws you in/keeps you hooked, and the candy shell helps to protect these contents with a nice functional layer.

Additionally, coming from a graphic design background, and now moving into cybersecurity, I feel as if I'm getting a glimpse at quite a few of these aspects. I've thoroughly enjoyed this class and look forward to learning more about JS, PHP, and Python as they relate to web applications and web design/functionality (as well as server-side!). This class has helped bridge various interests.
